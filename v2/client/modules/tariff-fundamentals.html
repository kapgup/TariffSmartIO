Introduction to Tariffs

Tariffs are taxes imposed by governments on imported goods and services. They serve as a key instrument of trade policy and have been used throughout economic history to influence trade patterns, protect domestic industries, and generate revenue.

Key Concept

A tariff is a tax on imports or exports between sovereign states. It is a form of regulation of foreign trade and a policy that taxes foreign products to encourage or safeguard domestic industry.

What Are Tariffs?

At their most basic, tariffs are customs duties that are levied on merchandise imports. They are typically charged as a percentage of the transaction value (the price paid or payable for the imported items). Tariffs raise the prices of imported goods, making them more expensive to consumers. This can encourage domestic production by giving a price advantage to locally-produced goods over similar imported items.

Brief Historical Context

Tariffs have been used throughout economic history:

  • In ancient times, duties were collected on goods entering city-states
  • During the Middle Ages, cities and feudal lords imposed duties on merchants traveling through their territories
  • In the 17th-19th centuries, mercantilist economic theories encouraged high tariffs to maximize exports and minimize imports
  • The early 20th century saw high tariff barriers before and during the Great Depression
  • Post-WWII, a general movement toward trade liberalization and tariff reduction began through GATT and later the WTO
  • The 21st century has seen both continued liberalization and occasional protectionist measures

Example

If Country A imposes a 20% tariff on automobiles imported from Country B, and an automobile costs $20,000 before the tariff, the importer would need to pay a $4,000 tariff duty. This cost is typically passed on to consumers, making the imported automobile cost $24,000.
